To Those Who Left Us

I’ve been abandoned three times in my life.  Twice by my mother, and once by my church fellowship.  Abandonment is a broad topic, and it actually happens all the time, in our friendships and our business dealings.  These are the three that have effected me the deepest.

I’ve remained with my pastor since I was 18, and I’ve attended his meetings religiously for almost three decades now.  In that time, our congregation has shrunk from a small church with no more than 50 people attending our weekly events at any given time, and now we are down to no more than three small families in attendance for 99% of our weekly events, and that includes the pastor’s wife and their cat.  Not even my pastor’s own children have continued to attend his weekly services once they reached adulthood.  Nor has my eldest.

Several years after knowing and working with my pastor, I began to think of him as my dad.  I could spend some time talking about the amazing friendship that we built, but that isn’t the purpose of this essay.

I love my little brother, and I love the gatherings he has at his home every Saturday evening.  He invites basically everyone he knows in the region to sit around, eat good food, and fellowship.  That’s all I ever wanted to do with Sunday morning.  I like to talk with my friends, and sometimes I like to get a little deep, if I trust the friends I’m with.  And good conversations sometimes need a long introduction, a little patience, and the ability to listen as well as I speak.  These are the conversations I crave.  I think I’ve always craved them.

So I wish I could ask my brothers and my sisters, as I wish I could ask so many who have left our weekly attendance over the years:  Is this not the kind of conversation you’re seeking?  Do you not find our dad’s choice of topics interesting?  Do you feel like he dominates the room?

The last question is the only serious question.  I mean, the reason that you would deprive your mentor of so many more participants every Sunday morning, can’t possibly be because you’re tired from your own community gathering from the night prior?  I know it’s fun to party on Saturday, but I must respect you enough to seek a deeper answer.  

And now that I think about it, I can’t even entertain the third question anymore, because you know Dad as well as I do.  So that eliminates every reason that I’m capable of imagining, and every reason you have offered.  

It’s like an M.C. Escher painting.
