If the dog who growls must be obeyed, then the dog is in charge, not his master.  We must test our dogs purposefully, not by abusing them, but by annoying them with occasional rough play, so that we know if they think they’re in charge, and how far they’ll go to assert their dominance.  If the dog insists that they’re in charge to the point of violence, there can be only two outcomes:  Either they don’t get a home, or they become the master of their home.

Some phrases to consider:

That person must have had a good reason to stab you.

I’m sorry I burned your family’s home to the ground.  I was abused as a child.  Can we still be friends?

What did you do to provoke that man to beat you, and why can’t you forgive him?

We spoke last night about Duncan being a part of the family.  Imagine if one of you, my children or my wife, became annoyed with something I was doing, felt I was provoking you, moved quickly to anger, calmly pulled a knife from your pocket, shouted “FUCK YOU!”, and slashed me in the face.  How do you think I would feel, as I sat there enduring stitches to hopefully repair my face, as I processed what had happened one day at a time?  And if you had done that to your father, after calming down, how would you feel about your actions?  If you had been the one who slashed me, would you believe that there was any possible way forward between us?  Would you suppose that I would ever forgive you?  Could you ever forgive yourself?

How would you feel about your sibling, if they slashed your father in the face?
