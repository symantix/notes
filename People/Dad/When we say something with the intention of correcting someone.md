When we say something with the intention of correcting someone, it’s important that no look of scorn or judgement is on our face, and the statement itself must be very nonjudgmental, one of observation that could be interpreted both positively or negatively on the face of it.  If we fail to leave all expressions of judgement or scorn from our face, the recipient will project their own negative thoughts on us, concluding that this is our judgement and not theirs, and they will likely become dismissive and defiant, exacerbating the problem.  If we succeed, and our body language is unreadable, or if it is at least interpreted positively (not ideal, as they could possibly conclude that we are being sarcastic), then for any thoughts of judgement that arise in the recipient's mind, they will have to conclude that the thoughts arose from their own mind, unprovoked, and they will have to contend with those thoughts on their own.  This is the perfect place for the mind to be when self-correcting.

If the person still fails to self-correct, this approach must not be abandoned.  People don’t always self-correct under any circumstances, but they definitely do not self-correct when they feel that the process is forced on them by someone else.
