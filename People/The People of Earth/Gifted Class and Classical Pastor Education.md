In elementary school, I was placed into a class for “gifted” kids, which was a small group that gathered at least weekly.  It’s unfortunate that these classes were not simply called “school”, so that everyone could have had access to them, because in those classes, education was very stimulating, and many other classes were often boring by comparison.  These classes weren’t usually difficult to understand, but the teachers were creative and passionate, and we worked through interesting projects to understand new concepts and develop skills in the arts, technology, knowledge enrichment and self-educating, history, culture, and so on.

In my early adult life, I spent all the hours of my days in a classical education environment.  My teacher was one person, a pastor who learned how to read ancient Greek in the fourth grade.  All my life, I have encountered pastors, and I have pecked them to death with my questions.  This pastor has sometimes bled, but he is still standing.

How could I tell that this man aimed at truth while the religious communities around him floundered in superstition?  I had learned, in elementary school, and particularly in those “gifted” classes, what the voices of academia and the arts sound like.  I learned, in that amazing school with the open floor plan, to seek the presence of the educated and disciplined.  That’s where thought leaves the circular universe and begins to aim upward.
