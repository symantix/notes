Better Social Media Discussion Model

Here’s an idea I’ve had in the back of my mind for a while, for a better social media discussion model.

There was a time when Facebook and Twitter had the social media market cornered, with no chance of any competitor surpassing them.  However, with numerous frustrations concerning both platforms slowly mounting over the years, with a sharp upturn more recently, the market feels more open to alternatives.  This is leading to everyone and their dog building social media clones, with nothing new to offer except an improved user agreement.

There are several major problems with modern social media platforms, some more solvable than others.  I’d like to focus on two:

1. Over-simplified user wall.  Everyone looks the same except for their images.
2. Over-simplified discussion model that is vulnerable to chaos.

The first problem is easy to resolve conceptually, but will require some technical heavy-lifting:  User walls should be much more than walls; they should be entire websites, built modularly with drag-&-droppable components that all users to design and redesign their pages to suit their own personality.  Some people don’t want their social media posts all over their front page.  Some people want to highlight their images, or sell products.  Rather than forcing every user page to be a social media hub, we build a platform where any site can be built, and they all can talk to one another on our social media platform.  If they want their posts to be on their front page, we have a component that they can just drag onto their page.

The second problem requires less technical heavy lifting, but it may be what could truly set our social media platform apart:  a better discussion model.

All social media platforms have constructed their discussion models similarly:  Someone posts a thought, then everyone posts random comments on that thought, either directed to the poster or another commenter.  The discussions completely open.  The problem with this is that some people don’t understand how to have a constructive and focused conversation, and their input can quickly divert the topic or send the whole discussion spiraling into chaos.

A post can be about several different things.  It could be about a thought you had, or an image you like or created, or a link to an article, product, etc.  But what if a post could be about the evolving conversation itself, and instead of the commenters participating in that conversation, they are instead talking about it in the comments area, now separate from the focussed chat.  What if, for example, a scientist could pose a challenging question, invite a few colleagues to participate in an informed and productive conversation on the topic, and allow novices to speak their mind in the comments section, now separate.

For this, we need a better discussion model, permissions-based, where the users are divided into participants and spectators.  A user starts a topic, invites participants, and then specifies whether spectators can comment.  Comments go to the audience section, which could be collapsed/hidden by default.

Existing social media has a lot of great features.  We don’t need to re-invent the wheel here, but a couple of key improvements to our functionality may set us apart enough to get our foot well into the market.
